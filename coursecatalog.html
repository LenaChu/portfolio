COURSE CATALOG

ROLE

Competitive analysis, UX research, information architecture, UI design

MEMBER

Lan Li, Oliver Lu, Garrett Spencer

TIMELINE

Jan. 2019 - May. 2019

OVERVIEW

For students, planning courses is a complicated process. It involves a lot information collecting and desicion making. The current UT course catalog and registration process, however, is not adequate to support students' needs of learning more about courses and a straight way to complete registration. To address those issues, we combined course catalog, time table, and registration page into an integrated portal, creating an one-stop experience of course registration process. We also enhanced the information offered in course catalog. Through the brand new UTLife system, students can access course information more easily and more comprehensively, plan their schedule with visualized time table, and register classes painlessly.

In this project, we utilized serveral reserach methods, including competitve analysis, user interview, affinity diagram, content inventory, and card sorting. We then generated mi-fi mockups and ran one round usabiblity test. The tools we used were Adobe XD, Mural, and Google Sheets.

DESIGN PROCESS

BACKGORUND

Although it has importance to every UT student, unfortunately, the UT course catalog has long been complained for its outdated and lack of integration. The course search criteria used by the current website do not meet the need for more precise filtering. In addition, there is no further action students can take on the search result page, such as adding a course to planner or bookmarking. Students still have to take notes of the course numbers by hand and put those numbers on the registration web page, which is separated from the course search website.

Being the student of UT, we know those pain points well. It intrigues us to initiate a redesign project of the UT course catalog. On the other hand, from the perspective of Information Architecture, we are also interested in what and how students usually look for when composing their class schedule. We are going to take both content and user centered approaches to tackle those research questions.

PROBLEMS

Through our research, we found that UT’s course catalog and registration system doesn’t satisfy students’ needs. There are 3 major problems existing in the current system.

No efficient schedule planning tool

Inadequate informative support

Fragmented registration process

SOLUTIONS

In response to these 3 problems, we proposed the following solutions

Create task based supportive tool

Consolidate information source

Integrate course finding and registration

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

In order to learn more about how course catalog and register systems are like, and how they work, we firstly conducted a competitive analysis. We selected 15 institutions in and out of the United States, including UT. Through the competitive analysis, we found there are numerous approaches those institutions adopted to create course catalog systems. On register system, however, we had trouble to obtain access due to the exclusion of visitors. Because of this, the competitive analysis mainly focuses on course catalog. Though we were able to observe general registration procedures through tutorial videos universities provided.

The four major parts we analyzed are:

01

INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

Organizational system, navigational system, organizational structure, and metadata.

02

SEARCH FUNCTIONALITY

How the site designed the course search experience

03

VISUAL DESIGN

The overall refinement of visual design for the site.

04

ADDITIONAL FEATURES

Any outstanding features besides the bare minimum course search process.

TAKEAWAYS

The results of the competitive analysis yielded valuable clues of the general structure of course catalog and the variety of features that the institutions offer. Based on the analysis, we reached five major takeaways.

1. Majority of course sites utilized a form of hyperlinked and hierarchical organizational structure.
2. Metadata related to courses are similar across the university course catalogs.
3. The usability of course search varied widely. With search filters being the most common feature across all institutions, error recovery, alternative views, refine query, result action are not as evenly implemented across the board.
4. Timetable and course schedule visualization were some of the more common special features implemented by select institutions.
5. Visual design did vary, institutions with clean and organized design were also the ones with the most comprehensive set of features.

USER INTERVIEW

To better understand how our target users use the current UT course catalog, and what strategy they adopt to deal with the tasks of course search and class registration, we conducted 5 user interviews. All interviewees were UT students. Because the course catalog and registration system are used by students from all degrees, we covered a wide variety of students. 2 students are undergraduate, 2 are master, and 1 is PhD.

AFFINITY DIAGRAM

After compiling the interview records, we used affinity diagrams to extract the key ideas from the transcribed information. We came up with 140 sticky notes filling the feedbacks. We then grouped these 140 sticky notes into several clusters and evaluated them to further clarify the findings. We extracted 8 major buckets that we think most relevant to course planning and registration. Through the exercise, we concluded 4 takeaways.

Time table is an important schedule-visualizing tool.

The teaching style of the instructor for the course is highly considered.

Students often seek inner and outer information sources.

Interpersonal information exchange is heavily relied on.
